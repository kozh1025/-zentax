Q1
The manager ＿＿ the highest sales this quarter will be honored at the company's anniversary ceremony.
【中譯】其團隊本季締造最高業績的那位經理，將在公司週年慶典上獲得表揚。
A
whose team achieved
B
who's team achieved
C
which team achieved
D
that team achieved
解題關鍵
whose 是關係代名詞的所有格形式，可修飾人或物，結構為「先行詞 + whose + 名詞 + 謂語」。本句中 whose team = the manager's team，whose 後接名詞 team 作主語。
陷阱提示
最常見陷阱：who's 與 whose 發音相同但意思不同——who's 是 who is 或 who has 的縮寫，不可作所有格；which 與 that 均無所有格形式，不能直接修飾名詞。
文法補充
whose 為關係代名詞所有格，可修飾人（the employee whose performance...）或物（the building whose roof...）；注意 whose（所有格）vs. who's（who is 縮寫）的拼寫差異是 TOEIC 常考陷阱。
單字/片語詞性音標主要意思搭配用法
whose team achievedrel.pron./huːz/其團隊達到……（所有格關係代名詞）whose = 人/物的所有格關係代名詞 ✓
who's team achieved錯誤縮寫/huːz/who's = who is（縮寫誤用）who's 為 who is 縮寫，非所有格
which team achievedrel.pron./wɪtʃ/which 作主格/受格，非所有格which 不可作所有格修飾名詞
that team achievedrel.pron./ðæt/that 作主格/受格，非所有格that 不可作所有格修飾名詞

Q9
The revised compliance framework ＿＿ been issued last month, the department would not have faced the regulatory penalty it received in April.
【中譯】__ 修訂後的合規框架上個月已發布，該部門就不會遭受四月收到的法規處罰。
A
Were it not for the fact that ... had
B
Had the revised compliance framework
C
If the revised compliance framework had
D

解題關鍵
此題考 C1 虛擬倒裝結構 were it not for，表示「要不是因為某事（反事實）」；原句為 'Were it not for the fact that the framework had been issued..., the department would not have faced...'，後接過去完成的反事實結果句。
陷阱提示
Had + S + p.p. 為已禁用文法（倒裝 If 條件句）；were it not for 與 had 倒裝形式不同，前者針對名詞短語，後者針對動作條件。
文法補充
were it not for + N/NP = if it were not for = 要不是有…（現在/未來反事實）；were it not for the fact that + clause 可延伸至子句。注意：本題結果句用 would not have + p.p.，故條件為過去反事實。
單字/片語詞性音標主要意思搭配用法
Were it not for the fact that ... hadconj./wɜːr ɪt nɒt fər/要不是（虛擬語氣，強調反事實）Were it not for X, S + would not have + p.p. ✓
Had the revised compliance frameworkconj./hæd/若已…（倒裝假設）Had + S + p.p.（倒裝If條件句，已禁用）

The Rise of Direct-to-Consumer Brands and Its Impact on Traditional Retail Channels

The rapid growth of direct-to-consumer (D2C) brands has fundamentally altered the retail landscape over the past decade. By selling products directly to shoppers through proprietary websites and social media platforms, these brands bypass traditional intermediaries such as department stores and wholesale distributors. While this model grants companies greater control over pricing, branding, and customer data, it has placed considerable pressure on brick-and-mortar retailers that have historically depended on stocking third-party goods.

Traditional retailers have responded in several ways. Some have accelerated their own digital transformation efforts, investing in e-commerce infrastructure and data analytics to better compete with the personalized shopping experiences that D2C brands offer. Others have partnered with established D2C companies, offering shelf space or co-branded marketing initiatives in exchange for exclusive distribution rights in specific geographic markets. Industry analysts note that retailers who have been slow to adapt risk losing not only revenue but also valuable shelf-space leverage over suppliers.

For D2C brands themselves, the path forward is not without challenges. Customer acquisition costs through digital advertising have risen sharply in recent years, prompting some brands to reconsider their reliance on online-only channels. A growing number of D2C companies are now opening physical storefronts, not to replicate the traditional retail model, but to create immersive brand experiences that reinforce customer loyalty and drive online sales.
